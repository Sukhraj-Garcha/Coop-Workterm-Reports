About


NOTE: This section has the same content as last term

Bastian Solutions is a Toyota Advanced Logistics company that specializes in material handling, robotics integration, and providing automated solutions for distribution, manufacturing, and order fulfillment centers around the world. Bastian provides automation solutions in the following areas: conveyor/sortation, goods to person, automated storage and retrieval systems (ASRS), picking, and automated guided vehicles (AGVs). The company has too many automated solutions to go into detail about each item here, but you can explore all of their projects here. The bread and butter of Bastian’s automated solutions is their software known as Exacta. Exacta is broken into three main components: ExactaWCS, ExactaWMS, ExactaWES. ExactaWCS is the warehouse control system that facilitates material and information flow to order fulfillment technology and material handling equipment. ExactaWMS is a warehouse management system that tracks inventory, inbound and outbound traffic, and allows customers to customize and view their warehouse in ways that best benefit their requirements. ExactaWES is the warehouse execution system, which is a combination of WCS and MCS. In 1952, Elgan Stark founded the company that would eventually become Bastian Solutions (then known as E.C Stark Co. Inc. At this point, the company supplied conventional material handling products. In 1971, the company was renamed to Bastian Handling Corporation. In 1980, the company shifted their focus to become more involved with automated solutions and information systems that control the flow of products in warehouses and manufacturing facilities. Between the years of 2005 and 2012, four major changes occurred: Bastian created its first robotics division, the company started manufacturing its own line of conveyor, more offices opened up around the world (Toronto, Montreal, Atlanta, etc.), and began operation under the new name Bastian Solutions. The final major change to the company occurred in 2017 when Bastian Solutions was acquired by Toyota Advanced Logistics. Currently, Bastian Solutions has offices spread throughout Canada, the United States, and Mexico and operates with the main goal of providing clients a competitive advantage by designing and delivering world- class distribution and production solutions.

Job Description

This was my fourth (and fifth technically) term with Bastian solutions. The overall details of my work procedures (i.e. IDE, language, dev tools, etc.) remain the same as my previous terms. My main IDE for development is still Visual Studio and the team’s product is still built using C# and the .NET framework. However, something that was definitely different this time around was the complexity of work that I was doing. Compared to previous terms, in the past 8 months, I was given the opportunity to work on much larger and more difficult tasks (which I am extremely grateful for). Since there was a lot going on in the past 8 months (way too much to explain everything in detail), the rest of this section will highlight some of the biggest pieces/features that I worked on.

In my last term, I was working on a tool (mainly as a proof of concept) that would create a Unity asset for a warehouse shelving unit (aka a rack). At the end of that term, the tool was nothing more than a GUI that asked the user for the relevant information. There was no backend, no handshake with Unity, and definitely no assets being generated. Upon my return to Bastian, I was given the opportunity to continue working on this tool and developing it to completion. It was really exciting to be able to pick up where I had left off and see the idea through to its end. This task gave me the chance to improve multiple aspects of my development skills (designing the back end functionality, designing a GUI, development, and testing). This version of the tool allowed for the user to easily design a warehouse rack and was also capable of generating the corresponding visual asset in Unity (as a prefab). Overall, it was really cool to see the racking generator go from an idea, to a POC, all the way to a fully working tool.

Once the racking generator was complete, I was given the unique opportunity to act as a sort of “mentor” to our new co-op. I was tasked with handing off the tool to said co-op student, which involved teaching him how the tool worked, explaining the overall design, and explaining how the code works in detail. This was a very exciting task, especially since I had not done something like this before. I am very grateful that I had this opportunity since I think it's pretty rare for someone as junior as me to be acting as a mentor for someone.

After the tool had been handed off, I shifted my focus to working on more core pieces of our emulation. During this period, I was able to dive deep into the actual emulation engine and get a fundamental understanding of how it actually works. Additionally, I worked on cleaning up the eventing system between the emulation and Unity. This gave me the chance to improve my knowledge of how events work in C#, a concept I had always fumbled with. This task required me to dig through and understand the Unity side code (which had been written for our team by a third party). During this, the debugger became my best friend as I constantly found myself using breakpoints and stepping through the code to try and understand how the two pieces communicate with each other. In the end, I was able to take a confusing eventing system with an insane amount of unnecessary layers of abstraction and refactor it into a much cleaner, and easier to understand system. Another big part of this period of the co-op term was learning about our PLC system. This involved understanding how our “mock” PLCs communicate with the different pieces in a warehouse (i.e conveyors, AGVs, automated robots, etc.).

The last large piece of work that I was assigned was to design and implement a state machine that could be used to model the various flows of different warehouse systems. This was a challenging task because a lot of effort had to be put into the design phase to ensure that the implementation was efficient, but also applicable to any scenario we might possibly need. This task really gave me the chance to work on the entire development process since I had to start with a research phase to look at possible implementations of a state machine. Then I had to design (and re-design many times) the system, and of course, implement and test. I ended up spending many hours on call with teammates iterating over and improving the design to make sure it was as efficient and robust as possible. After the state machine was built up to a working stage, I started to work on system tests of very simple scenarios (eg. the state machine is representing a warehouse worker who is in one of these states: waiting, walking, working). This process involved setting up a state machine diagram to represent the workflow of the scenario, then implementing said diagram and updating/adding states if necessary. I thought it was really cool to use what are essentially NFA diagrams in a real world setting and can clearly see how useful they can be for modeling systems. The final part of this task was generating a state machine to represent an automated goods to person station. The steps to complete this were pretty much the same as the simple system tests, however, the complexity of this particular workflow was much greater. I had to spend a lot of time digging through documentation surrounding the G2P station in order to get a good idea of what possible states/paths we would need.

Conclusion

Another two terms with Bastian have gone and I could not be more sad about leaving the team. I am extremely grateful for how much I’ve had the opportunity to learn at Bastian and am honestly surprised at just how much my development skills have improved. Specifically, this term really built up my ability to read and understand other people’s code. Many of my tasks required me to either use someone else’s code to complete something or make changes to existing code, which is inevitable as a developer. Thus, I am thankful I had so many opportunities to work on this particular skill. Additionally, for both the racking generator and the state machine, I was able to experience the full design process before the implementation phase. This greatly improved my ability to think about creating a good base design before moving straight to implementation.

Overall, I spent 4 total terms with Bastian and I could not think of a better way to spend 16 months of working than with wonderful people on Team Bluejay. On a closing note, I just wanted to express how grateful I am to my manager, Sam, for giving me tasks that were challenging and that forced me to get out of my comfort zone of things that I already know how to do.
